Work

Sleep neuroscience lies at the core of my expertise - from sleep scoring and EEG/fMRI analysis to unraveling the intricacies of physiological signals. However, my skills extend well beyond this realm into the world of data science. I wield a powerful arsenal of time series modeling, signal processing techniques, statistical methods, and machine learning algorithms to extract insights from complex data. Computational modeling also ranks among my strengths, allowing me to develop simulations that capture the dynamic interplay of neural systems. Complementing this technical prowess is my versatility in programming languages like MATLAB, Python, R, Bash, and SQL. But my talents transcend mere number-crunching; I possess a knack for project leadership, organization, and communicating even the most abstruse concepts through engaging writing, visualizations, and illustrations. This multifaceted skill set positions me uniquely to bridge theoretical and experimental neuroscience, uncovering novel insights at the intersection of these realms.

RESEARCH EXPERIENCE

  • Spring 2019- present: PhD Candidate, Dr. Laura Lewis, Research Laboratory of Electronics, Massachusetts Institute of Technology
    Identified the arousal-state transition network involved in transitions from sleep to wake in humans using fast fMRI. Calculated how behavioral arousal state is locked to slow ongoing brain oscillations.
  • Fall 2018- Fall 2019: PhD Student, Dr. Nancy Kopell, Department of Mathematics, BU
    Simulated effects of anesthesia by manipulating metabolism rates in networks of pyramidal neurons and interneurons.
  • Spring 2018: Undergraduate Research Assistant, Dr. John Meitzen, Department of Biological Sciences, NCSU
    Employed computational neuroscience techniques to model the effects of estrogen on medium spiny neuron properties such as spine density and L-type calcium channel conductance.
  • Summer 2016-Fall 2017: Undergraduate Research Assistant, Dr. Alun Lloyd, Department of Biomathematics, NCSU
    Assisted in developing a method for detecting hidden nodes in neural networks using non-linear Kalman filtering.
  • Summer 2017: Summer Undergraduate Research Assistant, Dr. Reinhard Laubenbacher, Center for Quantitative Medicine, UConn Health Center, Farmington, CT
    Implemented network analysis and time series modeling of disease diagnoses from healthcare data as part of the Modeling and Simulations in Systems Biology REU Program.
